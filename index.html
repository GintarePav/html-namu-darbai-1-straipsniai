Feed Your Senses: A Review of "Little Shop of Horrors" (1986)

By Alexandra P. T. Veracity

"Little Shop of Horrors" is a captivating blend of dark humor, catchy tunes, and unforgettable characters. Directed by Frank Oz, this cult classic musical comedy follows Seymour Krelborn, a meek floral assistant, as he discovers a peculiar plant named Audrey II. Voiced by Levi Stubbs with sinister glee, Audrey II sets off a chain of outrageous events that are as hilarious as they are macabre. As Seymour's relationship with Audrey II grows, so does the plant's insatiable appetite for blood and fame. The puppetry work by the Jim Henson Company brings Audrey II to life with a tangible and almost tactile quality, making it a character audiences won't soon forget. Each interaction between Seymour and Audrey II is infused with tension and dark humor, creating a captivating dynamic that drives the film forward.

Supported by an ensemble cast including Rick Moranis, Ellen Greene, and Steve Martin, the film thrives on its quirky characters and infectious musical numbers. Ellen Greene's portrayal of Audrey, a lovable yet vulnerable character, and Steve Martin's scene-stealing turn as the sadistic dentist add depth and dimension to the story. The film's blend of humor, horror, and heart makes it a must-see for fans of musicals and dark comedies alike.

At its core, "Little Shop of Horrors" is a story about love, ambition, and the unexpected consequences of success. With imaginative visual effects by the Jim Henson Company, the film brings Audrey II to life in all its carnivorous glory. From the toe-tapping "Skid Row" to the soulful "Suddenly Seymour," the soundtrack is both catchy and memorable. Beneath its campy exterior lies a tale that's equal parts heartwarming and darkly comic, making "Little Shop of Horrors" a timeless classic that continues to captivate audiences with its unique charm.

At its core, "The Lobster" is a biting critique of contemporary dating culture and the pressures society places on individuals to conform to traditional relationship norms. Lanthimos' surreal and deadpan style creates an unsettling atmosphere that underscores the film's themes of alienation and existential despair.
